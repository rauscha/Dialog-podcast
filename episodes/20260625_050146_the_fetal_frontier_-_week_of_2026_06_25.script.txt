JUNO [attentive]: Clinical question first — what are they actually asking?
CASPAR [precise]: Whether severe brain injury after FLA follows a recognizable temporal sequence. Descriptive question, retrospective cohort with serial prenatal and postnatal MRI — right tool for it. It can characterize the arc. It cannot establish causation, and imaging protocols and follow-up intervals almost certainly varied across the cohort.
JUNO [noting]: So the design earns a descriptive answer. What does the arc look like?
CASPAR [delivering]: Early findings are periventricular hemorrhagic infarction — the acute hemodynamic consequence of the procedure, co-twin demise, or post-procedure vascular instability. What follows, over weeks to months, is cerebral volume loss and polymicrogyria. The injury isn't a single event; it's a sequence.
JUNO [pressing]: Weeks to months is a wide window. What does that mean for a family asking whether the brain is okay at two weeks post-procedure?
CASPAR [flat]: It means they may be asking too early. A single post-procedure scan tells you whether periventricular hemorrhagic infarction is present. It doesn't tell you whether cerebral volume loss or polymicrogyria will develop. Those findings take time to declare.
JUNO [following]: So the counseling has to include the arc, not just the early scan result.
CASPAR [agreeing]: Right. Instead of "we'll watch for brain injury," you can name the sequence — early hemorrhagic infarction, then potential late structural change — and give families a more honest picture of how injury matures on imaging. That's a different conversation.
JUNO [pressing]: Does Kunpalin tell us when to scan?
CASPAR [careful]: No — and I want to be precise about that. The arc is real enough to counsel around. Optimal timing and frequency for post-FLA MRI are not established by this paper.
