CASPAR [explaining]: A convolutional neural network — a CNN — scans for local patterns in small patches of an image. Edges, textures, shapes. Stack enough of those layers and the network builds up from edges to curves to shapes to objects, without anyone specifying what to look for.
JUNO [following]: And this worked in 1989 — but then nothing happened for twenty-three years.

That was the fuel. JUNO [defining quickly]: Overfitting is when a model learns the training data so precisely it fails on new data. Like a student who memorizes past exams but can't answer new questions. Dropout fights that.

JUNO [asking the obvious]: Couldn't you just make the memory bigger?
CASPAR [explaining]: Making the network larger didn't fix it structurally. The context window — the span of text the model can use when making a prediction — was effectively short because information decayed as sequences grew longer. A bigger recurrent network just gave you a bigger version of the same structural problem.
JUNO [picking it up]: In 2017, eight researchers at Google published a paper titled "Attention Is All You Need." CASPAR [adding the second consequence]: And it was parallelizable in a way recurrent networks weren't. A recurrent network processes words one at a time — each step depends on the previous one.
