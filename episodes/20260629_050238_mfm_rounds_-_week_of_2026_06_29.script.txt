CASPAR [direct]: Every post-stillbirth consult has the same question underneath it: is the recurrence risk coming from whatever caused the first loss, or does the history itself carry independent weight? Henricks asks whether that's the whole story. Based on this cohort, the answer appears to be no.
JUNO [focused]: And if the history adds something independent, you're having a different conversation at the preconception visit even when the workup comes back clean.
CASPAR [grounded]: Right. The design challenge is separating those two things — which is what makes the adjustment strategy the part worth scrutinizing before the effect size means anything.
JUNO [precise]: From AJOG, 2026: Henricks and colleagues, a cohort study examining subsequent pregnancy outcomes after prior stillbirth — quantifying the independent risk of recurrence.
CASPAR [methodical]: Observational cohort with confounder adjustment. They adjusted for maternal comorbidities, placental pathology indicators, demographic factors. That adjustment set is what earns any claim of "independent" association. What it can't reach: interval health changes between pregnancies, behavioral factors, differential access to care. Those are the unmeasured residuals.
JUNO [pressing]: So when they say "independent" — how much does that actually hold?
CASPAR [measured]: It holds well enough to change the counseling sentence. It doesn't hold well enough to anchor a new intervention protocol. This paper clears the first bar, not the second.
JUNO [following]: What does the effect look like?
CASPAR [direct]: Prior stillbirth elevates subsequent stillbirth risk after adjustment. The direction is consistent and the association survives accounting for the known confounders. The history isn't simply a proxy for disease burden — it carries its own prognostic signal.
JUNO [noting]: So tight management of the underlying condition doesn't fully normalize the risk.
CASPAR [affirming]: That's the counterintuitive piece. You can optimize glycemic control, treat the thrombophilia, manage the hypertension — the history still adds something. That changes what you say at the preconception visit.
JUNO [clinical]: The patient who found a cause, treated it, and wants to know if she's back to baseline.
CASPAR [precise]: The answer used to be "probably, if we've addressed the etiology." Now it's: even after accounting for what we can measure about the cause, the history adds independent risk. What this paper doesn't include is a defined surveillance protocol — frequency, modality, gestational age to initiate. None of that is answered here.
JUNO [dry]: The paper tells you to surveil more intensively but not how.
CASPAR [flat]: Correct. Worth naming that gap explicitly rather than implying the monitoring plan is more evidence-based than it is.
JUNO [pushing]: On the residual confounding — how hard is the bias argument?
CASPAR [working through it]: For unmeasured confounding to fully explain this result, it would need to operate consistently in the direction of elevated risk across a heterogeneous population with heterogeneous causes of the first loss. You can't invoke bias without specifying which way it pushes and how large it would need to be. Possible. Not obviously sufficient to erase an adjusted association this direction-consistent.
JUNO [concise]: Real caveat. Not a dismissive one.
CASPAR [confirming]: Name it, don't hide behind it.
JUNO [pivot]: Rounds — four other things this week.
CASPAR [leading]: From AJOG, 2026: Rondagh and colleagues, a multicenter retrospective study of monochorionic twins — placental anastomosis patterns and severe brain injury after single fetal demise. The number and caliber of arterio-arterial and veno-venous anastomoses correlate with severe brain injury in the surviving twin. Mechanistically intuitive — larger caliber shunts permit greater hemodynamic shifts at the moment of demise — now quantified across multiple centers.
JUNO [naming the limit]: The limitation is that anastomosis patterns are characterized post-demise. It describes what happened. It doesn't tell you how to identify high-risk configurations before demise occurs.
CASPAR [clinical hook]: For post-demise counseling and MRI timing, it gives you a mechanistic anchor. High anastomosis burden on placental examination strengthens the case for early and serial neuroimaging in the survivor. More specific than "it depends on the placenta."
JUNO [moving]: From AJOG MFM, 2026: Champion and colleagues, a retrospective cohort in pregestational diabetes — fetal abdominal circumference extremes and composite perinatal morbidity. Both small and large AC percentiles associate with composite morbidity. Tight glycemic control that suppresses macrosomia doesn't eliminate risk at the small end.
CASPAR [holding the line]: Single-center, retrospective, low confidence rating. The clinical question is right. The answer is preliminary. Don't revise your surveillance protocol on this.
JUNO [brief]: Worth watching for replication.
CASPAR [continuing]: From AJOG MFM, 2026: Mei-Dan and colleagues, a narrative review — spontaneous preterm birth as an independent maternal cardiovascular risk factor. The synthesis supports including spontaneous PTB history in long-term cardiovascular risk counseling, independent of the conditions that caused the preterm birth. Evidence score 0.3, heterogeneous primary data underneath it. The causal mechanism — shared vascular pathology, chronic inflammation, endothelial dysfunction — is plausible. Not established by this paper.
JUNO [landing the scope question]: The question it raises is structural. Who owns the cardiovascular follow-up conversation after spontaneous PTB? MFM at the postpartum visit? Primary care? Cardiology? The paper makes the argument for having the conversation. It doesn't assign the responsibility.
CASPAR [practical]: In practice the MFM is most likely to have it and least likely to have a clear handoff pathway. Put PTB history in the cardiovascular risk record. Operationalizing beyond that is downstream.
JUNO [last round]: From AJOG MFM, 2026: Tersigni and colleagues, a narrative review — the case for pre-cerclage amniocentesis in asymptomatic short cervix. The framework: in asymptomatic patients with mid-trimester short cervix, distinguishing mechanical from inflammatory cervical insufficiency via amniotic fluid cytokine profile and culture could identify patients where cerclage is futile or potentially harmful because subclinical intra-amniotic infection is driving the shortening.
CASPAR [precise]: Narrative review, no new primary data, evidence score 0.3. The hypothesis is clean. The trial hasn't been run. Procedural risk of amniocentesis in this context, and whether the diagnostic yield changes management often enough to justify it, are open questions. The mechanical-versus-inflammatory distinction already shapes cerclage candidacy informally at some centers. This paper argues for making that systematic. It doesn't establish that doing so improves outcomes.
JUNO [closing]: Three things still unsettled. Optimal surveillance after prior stillbirth — Henricks justifies the conversation, doesn't define the protocol. Anastomosis burden in monochorionic twins — can it be characterized antenatally, before demise? Rondagh describes the post-demise picture; the prospective question is open. And cardiovascular follow-up after spontaneous PTB — who owns it, how it gets operationalized — not answered by a narrative review.
CASPAR [brief]: Three named gaps. Better than three unnamed ones.
